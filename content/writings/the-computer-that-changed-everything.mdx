For those who don't know me yet, my name is **Rizky Aditya**. Today, I work as a GRC Professional and Security Researcher. But this story isn't about cybersecurity—at least, not at the beginning.

Everything started in 2012, when I was just seven years old.

One day, my parents gave me my very first computer. To be honest, I had no idea what a computer was supposed to do. I didn't know how to use it, what it was capable of, or why everyone seemed so excited about having one. To me, it was simply a mysterious machine with a keyboard, a screen, and a desktop full of strange icons.

Then one icon caught my attention.

It showed a police officer holding a rifle. Curious, I double-clicked it without thinking.

That single click opened **Counter-Strike 1.6**—a legendary first-person shooter that was incredibly popular around the world at the time. Of course, none of that meant anything to me back then. I was just a seven-year-old kid who wanted to see what would happen.

There was only one problem.

I couldn't understand anything on the screen.

Every menu, every button, every piece of text was in English, a language I had never learned before. I didn't know how to start a match, what the options meant, or even what I was supposed to do.

Thankfully, my uncle noticed my confusion and patiently showed me how to play. He taught me the basics, and after watching him a few times, I began memorizing where to click and what each button did. I still couldn't read English, but I didn't need to—I had memorized the game through repetition.

Before I knew it, I was completely hooked.

What started as curiosity quickly became something I looked forward to every day. I spent hours playing, exploring, clicking every button I could find, and wondering what else this machine could do besides running games.

Looking back now, I realize it was never really about Counter-Strike.

The game was simply the first door that computer opened for me.

Behind that door was a much bigger world—one filled with technology, endless questions, and opportunities to learn. I just didn't know it yet.

If someone had told that seven-year-old kid that one day he would help organizations manage cyber risk, perform penetration tests, and even report valid vulnerabilities to companies like Google, I probably would have laughed.

At that moment, I wasn't dreaming of becoming a cybersecurity professional.

I was just a curious kid trying to figure out what a computer could do.

And somehow, that curiosity never left.
